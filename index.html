how it works

The operation of an electromagnetic train using the concept of electromagnetic induction revolves around creating magnetic fields that allow the train to levitate and propel forward without physical contact with the track. An electromagnetic train operates using powerful magnets to lift and propel the train forward, eliminating the need for wheels and tracks. Electromagnets beneath the train create a magnetic field that repels the track, allowing the train to float above the rails. This reduces friction, enabling the train to reach extremely high speeds while providing a smooth, quiet ride.

Electromagnetic Levitation

Electromagnetic induction is used to create repulsive forces between the train and the track. Magnets on the track induce currents in the conductive coils or superconducting magnets on the train. This interaction generates opposing magnetic fields, lifting the train above the track (levitation), minimizing friction and wear.

Propulsion System

Electromagnetic induction also powers the propulsion of the train. Alternating current (AC) is passed through electromagnets embedded along the track, creating a changing magnetic field. This changing magnetic field induces electric currents in the coils of the train's magnets, producing magnetic forces that push and pull the train forward, similar to how an electric motor works.
